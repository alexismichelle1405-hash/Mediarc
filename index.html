How to use each section
1
Universities
Add each university you're considering. You can record their A-level requirements, typical UCAT scores, interview format, and your own notes. Use the status tag (Considering → Applied → Interview → Offer) to track where you are with each one. The Applications page gives you a summary table across all universities at a glance.
2
Portfolio
Log every relevant experience — work experience, volunteering, leadership roles, research, and skills. For each entry, write a description of what you did and a reflection on what you learned. Your reflections are the raw material for your personal statement, so the more detail the better. Aim for at least 8–10 entries before you apply.
3
Personal Statement
Use the PS editor under Personal Profile. Every time you click Save, a new version is created so you never lose older drafts. The character counter turns red if you go over 4,000. Use the Why Medicine and Wider Reading tabs to build out supporting content that feeds into your statement.
4
Networking
Keep a record of doctors, consultants, and other contacts you meet during work experience or shadowing. Note their role, where you met them, and any follow-up actions. These contacts can be useful for references or further shadowing opportunities.
5
UCAT Tracker
Log every practice test result. The tracker calculates your best score, recent average, and section averages so you can see which areas need the most work. Start logging from your very first practice test so you can track improvement over time.
6
Calendar
Add open days, UCAT registration and test dates, your UCAS submission target date, and any interview invitations. Your dashboard deadlines are calculated automatically from your entry year, but the calendar is where you track the specific events you've booked.
Tips & advice
Start your personal statement early
Most successful applicants start drafting in June or July, giving time for multiple rewrites before the October UCAS deadline.
Quality of reflection over quantity of experience
Medical schools want to see that you've learned and grown from your experiences — a thoughtful reflection on two weeks of GP shadowing is more valuable than a list of activities with no insight.
UCAT: do timed practice from the start
The UCAT is primarily a test of speed under pressure. Always practise with the official time limits, even from your very first session.
Research each university thoroughly
Admissions tutors can tell when applicants haven't researched their course. Know the teaching style (PBL vs lecture-based), clinical exposure timeline, and what makes each school distinctive.
